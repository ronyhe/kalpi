# The Bader-Ofer Method: Israeli Parliamentary Seat Allocation

This document describes the Bader-Ofer method (חוק בדר-עופר) used to allocate seats in the Israeli Knesset. The method is named after Knesset members Yochanan Bader and Avraham Ofer who proposed it in 1973.

## Overview

The Bader-Ofer method is mathematically equivalent to the D'Hondt method (also known as the Jefferson method). It replaced the previous "largest remainder" method, which favored smaller parties. The current method gives a slight advantage to larger parties.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Apply the Electoral Threshold (אחוז החסימה)

Calculate the threshold: `threshold_votes = total_valid_votes × 0.0325`

Any party that received fewer votes than this threshold is eliminated. Their votes are not counted in subsequent calculations.

As of 2015, the threshold is 3.25% of valid votes.

### Calculate the Quota (המודד)

The quota represents the "price" of one seat:

```
quota = eligible_votes / 120
```

Where `eligible_votes` is the sum of votes for parties that passed the threshold.

### Initial Seat Distribution

Each party receives seats based on how many full quotas they achieved:

```
initial_seats = floor(party_votes / quota)
```

This typically allocates 113-117 seats, leaving a remainder to distribute.

### Distribute Remainder Seats (Bader-Ofer Formula)

The remaining seats are distributed one at a time. For each seat:

1. Calculate the "price for next seat" for each party/alliance:

    ```
    price = floor(votes / (current_seats + 1))
    ```

2. Award the seat to the party/alliance with the highest price.

3. Repeat until all 120 seats are allocated.

This formula ensures that seats go to parties that "pay" the most votes per seat.

## Surplus Agreements (הסכמי עודפים)

Two parties may sign a surplus agreement before the election. When calculating remainder seats:

1. The allied parties are treated as a single unit for the "price" calculation.
2. Their combined votes and seats determine their joint price.
3. If the alliance wins a remainder seat, it goes to whichever allied party has the higher individual price.

Surplus agreements can only benefit parties - they cannot cause a party to lose seats.

## Special Rule: 60-Seat Limit

A party that reaches 60 seats (half the Knesset) without receiving 50% of the valid votes cannot receive additional remainder seats. This rule has never been triggered in practice.
